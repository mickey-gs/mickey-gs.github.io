Albert Einstein

Achievements

Einstein's contribution to physics is nearly unmatched. His theory of relativity is one of the two pillars of modern physics (alongside quantum mechanics), and his famous equation has become synonymous with academic brilliance; it is perhaps "the world's most famous equation", according to biographer David Bodanis. But it is not for his theory of special relativity or mass-energy equivalence that he received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921- he received it for his description of the photoelectric effect. His explanation of this phenomenon suggested that light behaved not only as a wave (the prevailing theory since Newton), but that it also behaved as a stream of particles (photons). These ideas, which were controversial in his time, have become standard in science.
Incredibly, all of these discoveries were published in the same year (1906), along with an explanation of Brownian motion. This is often referred to as his "annus mirabilis", or miracle year. That year, he was 26.
Later in his career, he developed theories of general relativity and graviation, and investigated the quantum theory of radiation, the basis of laser technology.
His theory of gravitational waves was proved to be correct in February, 2016, when they were first directly observed by researchers at LIGO.

Involvement in World War II

Einstein was born in the German Empire, but moved to Switzerland early in his adult life, obtaining Swiss citizenship. He would move back to the German Empire in 1914 to accept the post of driector at the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute for Physics- however, this institute was never established, due to the outbreak of World War I.
Hitler became Chancellor of Germany in February, 1933. At this time, Einstein was on a visit to the United States. It was clear to him that, as a Jew, it was too dangerous to return to Germany, leading to his eventual immigration to the US.
In 1939, physicists Leó Szilárd an Eugene Wigner approached Einstein to explain the possibility of atomic bombs, which they feared would be developed by the Nazis. Szilárd convinced Einstein to write a joint letter, urging the United States government of the importance of developing nuclear weapons before the Nazis. Contrary to his personal stance of pacifism, Einstein agreed, believing that the Nazis posed a great enough threat to warrant the development of atomic bombs. This contributed to the establishment of the Manhattan Project.
In 1954, one year before his death, Einstein expressed that he had made "one great mistake in [his] life"- his signing of the letter recommending the development of nuclear bombs to FDR. He said there was "some justification— the danger that the Germans would make them".
